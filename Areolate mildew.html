Managing Grey or Areolate Mildew in Cotton Crops: A Comprehensive Guide

Symptoms:

  • Irregular to angular pale translucent lesions bound by veinlets and grey powdery growth appear on the lower surface of the leaf.
  • Correspondingly, light green specks appear on the upper surface of leaves.
  • In severe cases, whitish grey powdery growth appears on the upper surface. The affected leaves dry up inward, turn yellow, and fall prematurely.

Survival and Mode of Spread:

  • The pathogen survives during summer in infected crop residues.
  • The perennial cotton plants and self-sown cotton plants also harbor the pathogen.
  • Primary spread is through conidia from infected plant debris.
  • Secondary spread is through wind, rain splash, irrigation water, and implements.

Favourable Conditions:

  • Wet humid conditions during the winter cotton season.
  • Intermittent rains during the North-East monsoon season.
  • Low temperature (20-30Â°C) during October-January.
  • Close planting.
  • Excessive application of nitrogenous fertilizers.
  • Very early sowing or very late sowing of cotton.

Management:

  • Remove and burn the infected crop residues.
  • Remove the self-sown cotton plants during summer months.
  • Avoid excessive application of nitrogenous fertilizers/manures.
  • Adopt correct spacing based on soil conditions and varieties.
  • Spray Wettable sulphur @ 400 g/acre or Chlorothalonil @ 200 g/acre or Difenaconazole @ 100 ml or Kresoxim Methyl @ 200 ml/acre or Tebuconazole @ 200 ml/acre or Propiconazole @ 200 ml/acre or Metiram 55% + Pyraclostrobin 5% WG @ 200g/acre or Azoxystrobin 18.2% + Difenoconazole 11.4% w/w SC 200ml/acre at 60, 90, and 120 days after sowing.
By implementing these comprehensive management strategies, you can effectively control and mitigate the impact of Grey or Areolate Mildew in your cotton crops. Regular monitoring, early detection, and timely intervention are essential for maintaining healthy and productive cotton fields.
